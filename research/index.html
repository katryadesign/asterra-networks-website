Three shared market patterns

  1. Dual-axis IA (capability × industry) is the default. Every SOFT player runs an industries hub; HARD pairs solution trees with market hubs. Validates the brief's "two sections + cross-links". Navarino (single-axis) is the lone exception.
  2. Trust is quantified, certified, placed early. Hero stat bands + ISO/IACS cert strips are standard. Caveat: cross-page stat drift at Marlink (99.9/99.99), NSSLGlobal (50/55/56 yrs), Stripe (500M/250M).
  3. Sales/support split; the contact page is the weakest link. Two of five HARD sites have no web form at all (Navarino, Speedcast); aspirational tier qualifies leads progressively (Stripe).

Three key differences

  1. Discovery tooling ranges from nothing to faceted search — no well-designed mid-size finder exists; they're absent or expert-jargon tools.
  2. Curation vs catalog — maritime players curate few pillars with narrative pages; conglomerates expose full catalogs behind filters. Asterra's 5 pillars sit firmly on the curation side.
  3. Static PDF proof vs interactive proof — HARD relies on brochure PDFs; aspirational players embed live proof (latency test, demo+estimator, "Under the hood", product tours).

Conclusions per criterion

  • C1 Discovery: only NerdWallet scores 5; the direct market maxes at 4 (IEC, jargon-gated) → biggest gap = biggest opportunity.
  • C2 Industry framing: Marlink leads (5), all ≥3 → table stakes, not a differentiator.
  • C3 Plain-language: aspirational + NerdWallet at 5; IEC's jargon (2) is the cautionary floor.
  • C4 Proof: Marlink/Cloudflare/Stripe at 5; IEC's lone stat (2) shows the floor.
  • C5 Certs/methodology: Marlink strongest (5) via ISO/IACS; maritime buyers expect this — must match.
  • C6 Consistency: nobody scores 5; Marlink lowest (2) via stat drift → universally under-managed → argues for a single-source proof block.
  • C7 Contextual contact: Marlink/Stripe/NerdWallet at 5; the losers are dead-end directories.
  • C8 Interactive validation: aspirational + NerdWallet at 5; HARD group all ≤2 → absent in our market → opportunity.

Overall read: the two halves split cleanly. Marlink is strong on trust (C4/C5) and contact (C7) but weak on discovery (C1), interactivity (C8), consistency (C6). IEC is the only in-market player with a real finder, yet jargon (C3) and thin proof (C4) undercut it. High scorers (Stripe, NerdWallet, Cloudflare) win by combining a guided/low-jargon path in + self-serve validation + consistent specific proof — the combination no HARD player achieves.

Section 03

UX Patterns

Five principally different paradigms for the combined task — different core mechanism each: interview / identity / attributes / experience / evidence.

PatternHow it worksWhere appliedFits whenDoesn't fit when
P1 · Guided recommender
system-led interview (C1,+C3/C7)		 A few plain questions → ranked personalized recommendation (pros/cons) + next step; user answers, system leads. NerdWallet CardFinder ▦; Stripe "Guide me"; Cloudflare "Get a recommendation" (reported); Betterment/Wealthfront. In-market only IEC approximates (facet filter) ▦ Catalog hard to self-map even for a domain expert; few encodable variables; buyer early/unsure. Portfolio tiny/obvious (overkill); fuzzy matching (wrong pick erodes trust); experts want direct access.
P2 · Segment/industry-led
"choose your world" (C2,+C5)		 Entry by who you are (industry/segment); destination re-frames solutions in sector language + sector proof. Discovery by recognition. Category default — Marlink industry submenu + shipping page ▦; Cisco/IBM/Siemens/Honeywell hubs ▦ ▦; Cloudflare verticals Same capabilities serve very different sectors; buyers self-identify by industry; you have sector proof. Sectors too alike (duplication); no sector proof → filler pages ▦; need cuts across industries.
P3 · Faceted filter + comparison
self-directed attributes		 User narrows a catalog by attribute filters and/or compares a shortlist on shared specs; no system recommendation. Siemens 2048-item catalog ▦; IEC facets ▦; NerdWallet "Add to compare" ▦; AWS/e-commerce filters Large catalog, comparable attributes; expert buyers; late-stage choice between known options. substantiated reliability (C4/C5/C6)		 Trust asserted but proven — quantified stats + certs + named-outcome cases + logos + editorial expertise, early and consistent. No concrete proof → vague claims; inconsistent claims (stat drift = unreliability). Reinforces trust; nothing for discovery alone.

Three mechanisms to port into Asterra

1. Plain-language Solution Finder

How: one opening question in the buyer's words → 2–3 essential questions (industry, need, region) → ranked solution/bundle with pros/cons + context-carrying "Talk to an expert"; no login/contact to see results.

Solves: the core pain — can't self-map a complex catalog; removes browse-friction & the jargon wall.

From: NerdWallet CardFinder ▦ — adopt its plain language, not IEC's COTP/COTM jargon.

2. Single-source "Proof Block"

How: one reusable, tokenized component from a single data source (no 99.9% here / 99.99% there) — stat trio (coverage/uptime/scale) + certification strip + 1–2 named-outcome case cards.

Solves: pre-contact trust with specifics and eliminates the stat drift at Marlink & Stripe.

From: Cloudflare quantified proof ▦; single-source rule motivated by Marlink drift.

3. Contextual reassuring inquiry

How: every solution page carries "Talk to an expert" → simple, single-step form pre-filled with solution + industry (both fields the brief specifies), plus reassurance ("routed to a regional specialist — reply within one business day") — no multi-step quote wizard (CLAUDE.md §6–§7).

Solves: converts the just-convinced user in context; avoids dead-ends (no-form directories, off-domain forms).

From: Marlink routed form ▦ + Stripe's clarity/reassurance ▦ — not its multi-step qualification, which the brief excludes.

Mechanism NOT to port — Expert faceted product catalog / spec-filter (P3)

IEC facets ▦, Siemens 2048-item catalog ▦. Why not: Asterra has ~5 curated pillars, not thousands of SKUs — little to filter; it presumes the user already speaks each pillar's internal vocabulary — which our buyer, technical in their own domain but rarely a specialist across all five pillars, often does not — and adds cross-domain jargon that worsens "which fits me?". Facets only pay off at conglomerate scale; the guided finder (Mechanism 1) does the job.

Section 04

Conclusions

Basis: CLAUDE.md (maritime-first; ~5 curated pillars; primary conversion = qualified inquiry from a solution page; Solution Finder in scope; no homepage quote CTA) + the sections above.

Direction

Primary → P1 Guided recommender

the in-scope "Solution Finder". Three reasons:

  1. Hits the unsolved pain & differentiates — of 15 sites only IEC has a real finder and it's jargon-gated; the rest force browsing. Already committed in CLAUDE.md §7.
  2. Asterra's shape is ideal input — ~5 pillars × a defined industry set = few, encodable variables (trustworthy recommendations); persona is technical in its own domain but rarely a specialist across all five pillars → guidance beats browse.
  3. Lands in the primary conversion, in context — finder ends in a matched solution + pre-filled "Talk to an expert" (Mechanism 3), no dead-end.

Backup (complementary) → P2 Industry-led

Category default; matches how buyers self-identify; built anyway as the Industries section. Carries more of the discovery load, relative to the finder, if (a) research shows sector-first self-identification, or (b) trustworthy matching logic can't be encoded, or (c) build sequencing defers the finder (build order TBD — §13). The Solution Finder stays the committed in-scope discovery feature (§7).

Ranks second: non-differentiating, and within a sector still leaves "which of our 5 pillars?" — complements, not replaces, P1; depends on real sector proof.

Does not fit → P3 Faceted filter

Small curated portfolio (little to filter); buyer technical but not fluent in every pillar's vocabulary; facets expose internal vocabulary (COTP/COTM); managed-service not cleanly attribute-comparable. Same conclusion as "mechanism not to port".

Complements, not alternatives: P5 (Evidence) is a mandatory trust layer under whichever discovery pattern wins; P4 (Interactive proof) is a strong optional enhancer — scope to 1–2 credible tools (e.g. a non-map coverage/uptime stat rail, not the out-of-scope interactive coverage map — §7).

Design decisions (adopt)

  1. Solution Finder as the signature interaction — plain-language industry × need, aspirational-grade presentation; returns solution pages/cards with inquiry CTAs, not a raw SKU list.
  2. Contextual "Talk to an expert" inquiry on every solution page — solution + industry pre-filled, reassurance line; avoid form-less directories, off-domain forms, label/URL mismatches.
  3. Single-source tokenized Proof Block — consistent stats + cert strip + named-outcome cases on every solution/industry page.
  4. Industry section as recognition-based entry (P2), cross-linked with solutions per the brief's "two sections + cross-links".
  5. One lightweight interactive proof (e.g. a non-map coverage/uptime stat rail) — the pattern HARD entirely lacks; the interactive coverage map stays out of scope per CLAUDE.md §7.

Open questions for PM / designer

  1. Contact model: the brief specifies a simple, single-step routed form (Marlink) — not multi-step qualification (Stripe) or per-segment directories (Speedcast). Open at v1: alongside the specified industry field, also pre-fill/route on solution-of-interest? Submissions to email vs CRM?
  2. Catalog depth: ≤ ~30 offerings → curation-first, Finder = guided filter; hardware/accessories listed → facets + product content model needed. Which — and does the Finder return pages or cards with inquiry CTAs?
  3. Support surface at v1: competitors treat support as a trust asset (Speedcast Support Center ▦) but fragment it (IEC ×3 subdomains). The brief defers the portal — 24/7 claim + contacts only, or a public FAQ/knowledge layer?
